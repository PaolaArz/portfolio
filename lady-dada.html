Lady Dada

An editorial article about the women of the Dada movement, researched, written and designed to breathe the aesthetic of its subject.

Concept

A movement that claimed to be revolutionary — did it recognise its women?

I chose the Dada movement because I believe breaking rules is the most powerful way to convey a message, and Dada did it better than almost anyone. But the more I researched, the more one question kept surfacing: did a movement that claimed to be revolutionary actually recognise the women within it?

The answer the article arrives at is no, and yes, and it's complicated. Women were there from the start, at the Cabaret Voltaire, in the circles, in the work. But they were footnotes. Collaborators. Partners of the famous men. The article gives them their own space: Hannah Höch, Emmy Hennings, Angelika Hoerle, artists who were doing the work, being sidelined, and occasionally fighting back.

Design Decisions

The layout breathes the atmosphere of its subject

  • Brief The layout should "breathe the atmosphere" of the subject, not imitate it, but be coherent with it.
  • Typography Mixed typefaces, varied scales, diagonal text, different type treatments per section, echoing Dada's typographic chaos without reproducing it directly.
  • Layout Multi-column grids that break their own rules. Text that runs vertically. Elements that collide. Deliberately unresolved.
  • Imagery Archival black and white photography combined with reproductions of Dada works, Duchamp's Fountain, Höch's photomontages, Hoerle's drawings.
  • Colour Black, white, and red. The only colour in the document is used for emphasis, names, quotes, key terms, making it feel urgent rather than decorative.
  • Content The article moves from the historical context of WWI Europe, to the birth of Dada, to its philosophy, to the central question, and then gives each woman her own section as a subject, not a footnote.
