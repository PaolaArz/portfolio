AI Agents Learning Paths

Two full learning paths designed from scratch to take Make users from zero AI knowledge to building production-ready agents.

Context

AI Agents were new territory — for Make and for its users

As AI became impossible to ignore, Make recognised early that it needed to position itself as a serious player in the AI space, not just an automation tool. This meant helping users understand AI broadly and incorporate it into their existing workflows.

When Make launched its AI Agents feature, there was no existing training for it, neither for beginners, nor for advanced users. The feature was new, the topic was complex, and the audience ranged from people with zero AI knowledge to experienced builders who just needed to learn how to use agents inside Make specifically.

The challenge wasn't just teaching a tool. AI Agents were unfamiliar territory for most users: conceptually hard to grasp, easy to misuse, and with no precedent inside Make's existing training library.

The Problem

Two layers of barriers to adoption

To identify the real barriers to adoption, I ran workshops with product and growth teams and worked directly with SMEs. Those sessions surfaced two distinct layers of challenges.

The first was broader: users were unfamiliar with AI in general, what it was, how it worked, and how it could add value to their existing Make workflows. Before they could think about agents, they needed to understand AI itself.

The second was specific to agents: users didn't know what AI Agents were, didn't understand how they worked, couldn't tell when it made sense to use them versus standard automation, and — critically — couldn't make the agent actually do what they wanted. That last point was the hardest to solve: getting an agent to behave reliably requires a kind of thinking most users hadn't developed yet.

The Solution

Two learning paths built for two completely different audiences

To address these barriers, I designed two full learning paths from scratch: one for users new to AI, one for experienced builders ready to work with production-ready agents.

The paths were built to teach the theoretical knowledge behind AI and AI Agents concepts, giving users the understanding of what to use and why. They also served a strategic purpose: positioning Make as a reference point in the AI space and drawing attention to Make Academy. Finally, both paths covered how to use these features effectively inside Make, with practical guidelines and good practices.

My Role

End to end, from workshops to delivery

I owned the full process: from the initial workshops with product and growth teams through to learning path design, content structure, storyboarding, and supporting final delivery. No brief handed to me, I identified the problem, defined the solution, and built it.

My Approach

Audience first, then structure

From the workshops I identified two completely distinct audiences that needed different programs. That shaped the decision to build two separate learning paths rather than one — a single path would have been too basic for advanced builders and too overwhelming for beginners.

For both paths I followed a consistent instructional logic: what something is, how it works, why you need it, how to use it in Make. This structure directly addressed the core pain point — users needed conceptual grounding before they could make good decisions about when and how to use agents.

The Foundation path was designed to take someone with no AI knowledge and give them enough understanding to start experimenting with confidence. The Advanced path assumed existing Make expertise and focused entirely on what it takes to build agents that actually work reliably in real production workflows, something that didn't exist anywhere inside Make's training library before this.

The 90%+ satisfaction rate is unusually high for technical content covering a complex and fast-moving topic. Beyond the numbers, the paths contributed to increased adoption of AI Agents among Make users.

The learning paths also reinforced Make's positioning as a serious player in the AI space, not just an automation tool.
