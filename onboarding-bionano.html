Bionano

Building a Training Function from Scratch

Identifying three simultaneous gaps in a complex genomics company and building the training infrastructure to address all of them.

Context

No training infrastructure, a genuinely complex technology

When I joined Bionano, there was no training infrastructure. Customer training was handled by the support team as needed, mostly slide-based, with no instructional design behind it. There was no internal training on scientific topics, and no structured support for the pre-installation process: just a series of confused meetings with clients, sales teams, and distributors trying to navigate complex data storage, IT setup, and safety questions without any shared reference material.

Bionano's technology, Optical Genome Mapping, was genuinely complex. Customers needed to understand it scientifically, technically, and operationally before they could use it successfully.

The Problem

Three simultaneous gaps, none of them small

Three separate gaps existed simultaneously. Customers lacked structured onboarding that could take them from first contact to confident use of the technology. Internal colleagues had no training on the scientific topics underpinning the product. And the pre-installation process was a bottleneck: nobody had clear answers to the questions that consistently came up before a system could be installed, which slowed everything down and created anxiety on both sides.

The Solution

A training function built from nothing

I identified all three gaps and built a training function to address them, proposing each initiative, defining the format, and building the materials. For the pre-installation gap, I set up a working group with sales to systematically map the recurring questions and create material that could be used across customers, distributors, and partners.

My Role

Every project, end to end, without a precedent to follow

I owned every project end to end, from identifying the need and proposing the solution, to content design, production, and delivery. My background in biology and bioinformatics was essential: without it, I couldn't have designed training on Optical Genome Mapping, Fragile X, or FSHD to the level of accuracy and depth these audiences required. For customer training I also delivered face-to-face sessions directly. All of this was built without an existing framework, team, or precedent to follow.

My Approach

Each audience got a different format

Internal science training

Live sessions on complex scientific topics including Fragile X analysis and FSHD, designed following Gagné's Nine Instructional Events to ensure attention, clear objectives, active recall, and transfer to practice. Each session came with a facilitator guide and handover materials so the training could be reused after delivery.

For colleagues who couldn't attend live, asynchronous material was provided including interactive slides with animation and voiceover.

Where the topic was particularly abstract, like conditional probability in the Fragile X statistics module, I used scenarios to make the concept tangible: learners placed bets on a Formula 1 race, then received new information round by round to experience how probability shifts with context.

Slides — Fragile X internal science training

A selection of slides from the session, covering the diagnostic classification system, the conditional probability scenario used to open the statistics module, and the OGM assembly workflow.

Customer training

Face-to-face technical sessions delivered directly to customers, supported by a facilitator guide structuring the order of topics and what was needed for each session. To support customers between and after sessions, I also produced asynchronous performance support materials: infographics for software updates and hand-drawn "How To" cheatsheets explaining specific software features, visual, action-oriented, designed to be used directly at the bench without needing to contact support.

Training was evaluated using the first two levels of the Kirkpatrick Model, measuring learner reactions, knowledge transfer, confidence, and attitudes.

Infographic — Software update

Hand-annotated release notes translating technical patch details into plain-language highlights for lab users.

How To cheatsheets — software features

Hand-drawn step-by-step references for specific actions in the genome browser view, designed to be used directly at the bench.

Pre-installation support materials

A structured cheatsheet for sales teams and distributors covering data storage, IT setup, and safety questions, plus infographics showing the technical setup of computers and data transfer requirements. Designed to give anyone going into a first client meeting the answers they needed immediately, without escalating to support.

Infographic — SAPHYR system IT setup

Visual overview of the full SAPHYR system architecture: hardware, software, network, data storage, and safety considerations — designed for use in pre-installation meetings.

Cheatsheet — Data storage and safety questions

Structured reference covering the most common pre-installation questions on data storage, access, cloud options, and safety — one page, ready to use in any first client meeting.
